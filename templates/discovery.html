How We Find Exoplanets

Transit Photometry

Detects periodic dimming of starlight as planets pass in front of their host stars. This method has discovered over 70% of known exoplanets and is used by Kepler and TESS missions.

Success Rate: 4,234 discoveries
Best For: Earth-sized to Jupiter-sized planets

Radial Velocity

Measures the wobble of stars caused by gravitational pull of orbiting planets. This was the first method to detect exoplanets around sun-like stars.

Success Rate: 1,026 discoveries
Best For: Massive planets close to their stars

Gravitational Microlensing

Uses gravity as a lens to magnify light from distant stars, revealing planets when they pass between us and background stars.

Success Rate: 178 discoveries
Best For: Planets far from their stars

Direct Imaging

Directly photographs exoplanets by blocking out the bright light of their host stars using advanced coronagraphs and adaptive optics.

Success Rate: 69 discoveries
Best For: Young, hot, massive planets

Recent Groundbreaking Discoveries

NASA Exoplanet Missions

Kepler Space Telescope

Revolutionary mission that discovered over 2,600 confirmed exoplanets by monitoring 150,000 stars for transit events. Kepler showed us that planets are common in our galaxy.

Status: Mission Complete
Discoveries: 2,662 confirmed exoplanets
Legacy: Proved Earth-sized planets are common
2009-2017

TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite)

All-sky survey mission searching for exoplanets around the brightest stars near Earth. TESS is finding planets perfect for follow-up atmospheric studies.

Status: Active Mission
Discoveries: 402+ confirmed exoplanets
Goal: Survey 85% of the sky
2018-Present

James Webb Space Telescope

The most powerful space telescope ever built, capable of analyzing exoplanet atmospheres and searching for signs of habitability and life.

Status: Active Mission
Focus: Atmospheric characterization
Capability: Detecting water vapor, oxygen, methane
2021-Present
